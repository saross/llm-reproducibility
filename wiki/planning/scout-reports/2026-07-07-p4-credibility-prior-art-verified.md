# Prior-art report: LLM-automated computation of repliCATS-style credibility signals for HASS

## Executive summary

*(Verbatim from proposer's draft.)*

This is a **partially-solved landscape with a genuinely open niche in the middle**. Three adjacent territories are well-established: (a) human structured-elicitation platforms for replicability judgement (repliCATS/IDEA protocol, prediction markets), (b) text-based ML classifiers trained to predict replication outcomes (DARPA SCORE-era MACROSCORE, Yang/Youyou/Uzzi), and (c) a fast-growing 2025–2026 wave of LLM-based paper-quality/peer-review assistants and fraud auditors. None of these three territories has been fused into what your pipeline does: **using an LLM to *compute* repliCATS-style structured credibility signals from a structured claims-evidence-methods extraction of a paper, with quality gating and multi-run stability testing, for HASS discipline output.** I found no code, paper, or hosted service that does this combination. The closest analogues are (1) a 2026 Center for Open Science project (ReplicatorBench/ReplicatorAgent) that has LLM agents *actually re-execute* replications for social/behavioural science rather than score credibility signals from extractions, and (2) a Claude-Code multi-agent fraud-audit skill (`gunting`) that is architecturally similar (multi-agent LLM pipeline, benchmarked, stability-adjacent) but targets fraud detection, not replicability/credibility scoring, and is discipline-agnostic rather than HASS-specific. SciScore and the automated statistical-integrity detectors (statcheck, GRIM, Problematic Paper Screener) remain firmly biomedical/psychology-quantitative and do not extend to archaeology or HASS more broadly. Grant positioning can credibly claim: first LLM-automated computation of repliCATS-derived credibility signals; first application to HASS/archaeology; and a stability-testing methodology (25-run) that is more rigorous than anything published in this specific sub-area, though general "LLM output stability" methodology exists elsewhere.

## Recommendations

*(Verbatim from proposer's draft.)*

- **Use directly**: None of the 22 candidates is directly adoptable as a dependency for your pipeline as-is. The closest to "use directly" is **statcheck** (#16) as a supplementary rule-based signal-check *if* your archaeology corpus contains NHST-style statistical reporting worth auditing — but this will be the exception rather than the rule in HASS papers.

- **Adapt approach**:
  - **ReplicatorBench/ReplicatorAgent (#6)** — adapt its LLM-as-judge validation pattern (benchmarking agent output against expert-annotated ground truth) for your own stability-testing and validation story; also worth contacting the COS team, given shared DARPA-SCORE lineage and Open Science funder overlap.
  - **gunting (#13)** — adapt its independent-replication check (rerunning the benchmark with a different, offline, non-Claude model to rule out leakage) as a template for strengthening your own 25-run stability testing; also worth citing its benchmark-scorer design (AUC/F1/ECE/bootstrap CIs) as prior art for how to report calibration.
  - **PASTEL (#10)** — the "LLM extracts many small credibility signals → weak-supervision aggregation" architecture is directly analogous to what you're doing with Seven Signals; worth citing as the nearest published *pattern*, explicitly noting the domain difference (misinformation vs. academic replicability).
  - **aggreCAT (#1)** — cite (and potentially reuse the R package directly, MIT-licensed) for the mathematical aggregation step if your pipeline currently hand-rolls signal combination; this is purpose-built repliCATS aggregation maths from the original team.
  - **Scalable Delphi (#20)** and the **COVID-19 preprint paper (#22)** — cite both as the two ends of the "LLM replaces/augments Delphi-style elicitation panel" spectrum: one shows LLM panels work for structured elicitation (different domain), the other shows human structured elicitation predicts real replication outcomes for HASS-adjacent claims (no LLM). Your project sits in the unoccupied intersection.
