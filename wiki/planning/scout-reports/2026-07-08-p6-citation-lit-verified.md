## TL;DR

The literature splits cleanly into five traditions of very uneven maturity: citation-intent/function classification (mature, crowded, CS/ACL-owned), citation-accuracy/quotation-error empirical studies (mature but almost entirely biomedical), citation-distortion/selective-citation (a small, deep, biomedical seam now being automated), missing-literature/coverage evaluation (immature, CS-preprint-dominated, no HASS work), and HASS/humanities citation-practice scholarship (a separate, largely qualitative literature that barely touches the computational strands). Top-3 must-reads for your specific extension: **Sarol, Schneider & Kilicoglu 2025** (10.1002/pra2.1281 — the closest existing template: an NLP replication of citation-distortion detection; already in your Zotero), **Huvila, Andersson & Sköld 2022** (10.47989/irpaper941 — the only genuinely archaeological citation-function/paradata study), and **Greenberg 2009** (10.1136/bmj.b2680 — the conceptual scaffold of bias/amplification/invention). Biggest gap: no one has combined citation-fairness + accuracy + coverage + discourse-level engagement on a claims–evidence–methods graph, and the entire coverage/discourse side is empty for HASS and archaeology — that intersection is yours to define.

## Landscape

The field is really five conversations that mostly do not cite each other:

**A — Citation intent/function classification (mature, crowded, CS-owned).** A continuous ACL/NLP lineage runs Teufel 2006 (#1, function taxonomy) → Jurgens 2018 (#3, citation frames at field scale) → Cohan 2019 (#2, SciCite dataset/model) → Lauscher 2022 (#4, MultiCite: multi-sentence, multi-label). Post-2022 the work is heavily incremental — graph embeddings (#6), multi-task learning (#5), and 2026 architecture tweaks (#8) chasing marginal F1 gains on the same SciCite/ACL-ARC benchmarks. The interesting frontier is *domain adaptation* (#7 astrophysics), because that is where the community admits the general classifiers underperform. For your purposes this cluster is a solved-enough front end, not a research contribution — the open problem is representing its output in a graph, not building another classifier.

**B — Citation accuracy / quotation error (mature, deep, almost entirely biomedical).** A 40-year empirical tradition of hand-checking whether citing statements are supported by cited sources, consolidated in two meta-analyses (Jergas & Baethge 2015 #9; Baethge & Jergas 2025 #13) reporting quotation-error rates around 11–25%. The 2026 turn is toward scale and automation: the Lancet fabricated-citation audit (#12, 2.5M papers) and the first LLM-based quotation-error detector with interpretability (#14). This cluster owns the *ground-truth definitions* (major/minor, content/source error) you would need to label an accuracy task — but it has essentially never been run on HASS or archaeology.

**C — Selective citation / distortion / bias (small, deep, being automated right now).** Greenberg 2009 (#15) is the anchor; Urlings/Duyx 2021 (#16) is the rigorous multi-network quantification; and Sarol, Schneider & Kilicoglu 2025 (#17) is the moment this seam goes computational (an NLP replication of Greenberg). This is the most methodologically relevant cluster to your "does this paper cite fairly?" ambition and it is small enough to read exhaustively.

**D — Missing-literature / coverage / novelty (immature, CS-preprint-dominated).** Thin on peer-reviewed, DOI-bearing work: structured related-work generation with novelty statements (#18) and clinical-trial prior-evidence completeness (REVEAL, #19). The live action — "recommending missed citations identified by reviewers", NovBench, novelty-assessment agents — is almost all 2024–2026 arXiv (see Gaps). No HASS instances exist.

**E — HASS/humanities citation practice + discourse (a separate, older, qualitative literature).** Hyland 1999 (#20) and Hellqvist 2010 (#21) establish that humanities citation is context-bound, includes non-academic sources, and is not simple "acknowledgement of prior research" — which undercuts the assumptions baked into clusters A–D. Heibi & Peroni 2022 (#22) is the rare bridge (computational in-text citation analysis *of* the humanities), and Huvila 2022 (#23) is the archaeology-specific instance. This cluster is where your comparative advantage lies and where clusters A–D have no presence.
