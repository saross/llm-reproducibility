# Lit-scout report: Automated & LLM-based FAIR / open-science compliance assessment — literature map (with HASS/archaeology emphasis)

## TL;DR

The field splits into a mature **automated-FAIR-tooling / maturity-model** core (Wilkinson, F-UJI, Bahim RDA model) that is now being interrogated by a small but pointed **validation-and-disagreement** literature (Peters-von Gehlen's five-tool ensemble showing manual > automated scores; Candela's "FAIR Assessment Conundrum"), while a **parallel transparency-indicator-detection** tradition (Serghiou/rtransparent, ODDPub, PLOS Open Science Indicators) does at literature scale what you propose to do for FAIR — but almost entirely with rule-based text-mining, not LLMs. The three must-reads for positioning your lane are **Peters-von Gehlen et al. 2022** (`10.5334/dsj-2022-007`, the validation-design template), **Serghiou et al. 2021** (`10.1371/journal.pbio.3001107`, the scale-detection template and IV analogue), and **Marwick 2025** (`10.1016/j.jas.2025.106281`, archaeology reproducibility reviews at scale). The biggest gap you can productively fill: **LLM-based scoring of GO-FAIR sub-principles validated against human raters is essentially unpublished** — the only LLM+FAIR prior art (FAIR-GPT, the PFAS LLM paper, Fair-Way) is tool-building, not validated measurement, and none is in HASS/archaeology.

## Landscape

The territory has five interlocking conversations, only some of which the companion tooling sweep touched:

1. **Automated FAIR tooling and maturity models (crowded, mature).** Wilkinson's FAIR principles (2016) and the FAIR maturity-indicator/Evaluator framework (2019) anchor a now-dense tool ecosystem: F-UJI/FAIRsFAIR (Devaraju & Huber), the RDA FAIR Data Maturity Model (Bahim et al., the harmonisation layer that decomposes FAIR into the discrete indicators your "15 binary sub-principles" descend from), plus FAIR-Checker, FAIR Enough, FAIR Evaluation Services. This is well-trodden; your contribution is *not* here.

2. **Validation and disagreement between evaluators (small, high-value, under-exploited).** This is the crux for your validity claim. Peters-von Gehlen et al. (2022) applied *five* evaluation approaches (two automated, two manual, one hybrid) to the same climate datasets and found manual approaches scored systematically higher than automated ones, with tools agreeing more when they shared a manual method — the empirical demonstration that "automated FAIR score" is tool-dependent, not a ground truth. Candela, Mangione & Pavone (2024) generalise this into a critique of tool/metric non-comparability. Peng et al. (2024) push toward machine-actionable harmonisation. This cluster is where an LLM-scored approach validated against human raters would land as a genuine contribution.

3. **Transparency-indicator detection at literature scale (mature in biomedicine, rule-based).** Serghiou et al.'s rtransparent (2021, PLOS Biology) and Riedel et al.'s ODDPub (2020) mine full text for data/code-availability statements across tens of thousands of papers; PLOS Open Science Indicators operationalises the same via DataSeer's NLP. Colavizza et al. (2024) close the loop by regressing sharing behaviour onto an outcome (citations) — structurally the same design as your FAIR-scores-predict-reproduction study, with a different outcome variable. Crucially, **this whole tradition is rule-based/classical-NLP, not LLM** — which is exactly the seam you are proposing to work.

4. **LLM-based assessment of FAIR/open science (nascent, sparse, no HASS).** The entire visible corpus is: FAIR-GPT (Shigapov & Schumm 2024, a ChatGPT *consultant* that outsources the actual FAIRness scoring to external APIs), the PFAS LLM-FAIR paper (Dong et al. 2026, LLM-assisted FAIR+environmental evaluation of contaminant databases), plus the tooling sweep's Fair-Way (CIKM'25), Patra et al. (arXiv:2503.15929) and Windhouwer et al. (SemDH 2025). None does *validated binary sub-principle scoring against human raters*; none is in archaeology or HASS.

5. **Discipline-level data-sharing / FAIRness studies, and archaeology in particular (steady, discipline-fragmented).** Cross-discipline prevalence work (Hardwicke et al. 2020 for the social sciences — manual, 250-article sample; Sixto-Costoya et al. 2025 for addiction research using an automated tool) sits beside a distinct **archaeology data-sharing line** built largely around Marwick: computational reproducibility (2017), the data-citation-as-incentive standard (Marwick & Pilaar Birch 2018), the "Open Science in Archaeology" manifesto (2017), and — most importantly for you — Marwick (2025), which reports a *year of reproducibility reviews across 10,000 articles*, the closest existing thing to your reproduction-outcome framing in the discipline. Around it: repository/infrastructure work (tDAR/McManamon; Kansa & Kansa on collaborative identifiers, PNAS), sub-field studies (Lodwick on archaeobotany), and a fresh 2026 wave (Beck et al. on publishing; Roberts Thompson et al. on the digital-curation crisis; Vaesen & Hussain's sceptical "Perks and Perils of Replicability").

Where you sit: your lane threads clusters 2, 3 and 4 — an **LLM scorer** (cluster 4) of **FAIR sub-principles** (cluster 1), **validated against human raters** (cluster 2), applied at **literature+repository scale as an independent variable predicting reproduction** (cluster 3), with an **archaeology/HASS** proving ground (cluster 5). No single existing paper occupies that intersection.
