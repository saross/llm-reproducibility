# Lit-scout report: LLM/agent-based computational reproduction and replication of published research (grant positioning)

## TL;DR

Agentic computational reproduction and replication of research is a **fast-moving, preprint-dominated, and rapidly crowding** space: at least fifteen benchmarks and systems have appeared since CORE-Bench (Sept 2024), the great majority as arXiv preprints, with the first peer-reviewed venue landings only now emerging (REPRO-Bench at ACL Findings 2025). Both benchmarks you flagged **exist and are verified**: CORE-Bench (Siegel et al. 2024, `10.48550/arXiv.2409.11363`) for same-code-and-data reproduction, and PaperBench (Starace et al. 2025, `10.48550/arXiv.2504.01848`) for from-scratch replication — the two structural anchors of the field. The must-reads for positioning are those two plus **REPRO-Bench** (`10.18653/v1/2025.findings-acl.1210`), which moves the frontier into *social-science* reproducibility assessment. **Biggest gap: archaeology and the humanities are unoccupied by LLM-agent reproduction.** The social sciences are now actively contested (REPRO-Bench, ReplicatorBench, "Read the Paper, Write the Code", the Institute for Replication's AI Replication Engine), but no LLM-agent reproduction work targets archaeology/HASS-humanities — that discourse remains human-led (e.g. "Sharing Research Code in Archaeology").

## Landscape

The field splits cleanly along the **reproduction vs replication** axis you asked me to preserve:

- **Computational reproduction (same code + same data; re-execute and match).** CORE-Bench (#1) is the anchor — 270 tasks over 90 papers spanning computer science, social science, and medicine. This lineage now includes ClaroAI-Bench (#8, biomedical, with a five-dimension D1–D5 findability/accessibility/code/environment/results rubric that overlaps your FAIR-scoring scope), LMR-Bench (#9, language-modelling code), the materials-science study (#14), and — crucially for you — REPRO-Bench (#3), which reframes the task as *assessing* reproducibility of social-science papers rather than just re-running them.
- **Replication from scratch (rebuild the pipeline from the paper alone).** PaperBench (#2) is the anchor — 20 ICML 2024 papers, author-approved rubrics, ~8,300 atomic grading nodes, LLM judge. SciReplicate-Bench (#5), ResearchCodeBench (#10), ReplicationBench-astrophysics (#12), NatureBench (#13), the LLM Speedrunning benchmark (#16), and the broad AstaBench suite (#15) all sit here, each carving out a discipline or a slice of the pipeline.

Two structurally central non-benchmark works surfaced by chaining: **AI Agents That Matter** (#19, Kapoor/Narayanan — the same Princeton group behind CORE-Bench; a methodological critique of agent evaluation that every benchmark now cites) and the **Evaluation and Benchmarking of LLM Agents survey** (#20), which appeared in both the CORE-Bench and PaperBench forward chains (2 chains) and is the field's current review.

**Grey literature you must know about for positioning (no DOI, so not in the table):** the **Institute for Replication (I4R) "AI Replication Engine"** — a multi-agent system (Reproducibility, Error-Detection, Robustness agents; Llama 4 / Qwen 3 / GPT-5 / Claude) explicitly aimed at *social-science and economics* reproduction. Per I4R's own posts it has an active **SSHRC Insight Development Grant application (~CAD 95k, 2026–2028)**, plans evaluation over 250+ I4R Games papers and ~430 World Bank documents, and targets a NeurIPS 2026 submission. This is the closest thing to a direct competitor for a HASS-adjacent grant, and it is social-science-facing, not archaeology-facing. (Sources: i4replication.org blog; bitss.org.)
