# Prior-art report: Automated Research Design/Methods/Protocol Extraction and Methodological Quality Assessment

## Executive summary

The landscape is **partially solved, but only for one discipline and only for one half of the problem**. Statistical-integrity checking (statcheck, GRIM/GRIMMER, SPRITE/rsprite2) and reporting-guideline *compliance* checking (CONSORT-NLP, ARRIVE checker, SciScore, rtransparent) are mature-to-middling tools, but every one of them is built for structured biomedical/clinical-trial reporting (APA-style statistics, IMRaD sections, RCT checklists) and detects **presence/compliance**, not **appropriateness**. Methods-section information extraction exists mostly as isolated NLP tasks (PICO extraction, entity recognition over methods text) rather than as a full research-design/methods/protocol hierarchy, and is again clinical-trial-centric. Study-design classification tools exist (mostly RCT-vs-not binary classifiers) but nothing classifies the plural, often-nested, often-implicit designs typical of fieldwork disciplines. Crucially, I found no tool — open-source, commercial, or published-but-uncoded — that extracts research designs/methods/protocols from Humanities, Arts and Social Sciences (HASS) or fieldwork-based (archaeological, ethnographic) full-text papers, and no tool that judges methods **appropriateness** rather than mere presence. Two very recent methodological papers (Tan & D'Souza 2026; Simmons et al. 2025) independently confirm that current LLMs still struggle with exactly the kind of relational, cross-document, narrative-methods extraction the user's system performs — useful validation that the problem is genuinely hard, not merely unattempted.

## Recommendations

**Use directly:**
- None. Nothing in this list is a drop-in fit for HASS/fieldwork RDMAP extraction or appropriateness assessment. GROBID (#18) is worth adopting as *infrastructure* if the extraction pipeline ever needs PDF→structured-XML conversion at scale, but that's a different layer to what `research-assessor` already does via direct PDF reading.

**Adapt approach:**
- **rtransparent** (#7) — its regex/phrase-detection approach for transparency indicators in narrative full text is a plausible lightweight pattern for a "signal-detection" pre-pass ahead of LLM extraction, and its QUEST Center provenance is a strong credibility citation for a grant.
- **chanzuckerberg/full-text-mining-ner** (#17) and **section_specific_annotation_of_PICO** (#16) — both demonstrate section-aware, whole-paper (not abstract-only) methods-entity extraction; their annotation schemas are worth a closer read even though the domain (biomedical) and mechanism (fine-tuned NER, not LLM prompting) differ from the user's approach.
- **F-UJI** (#23) — the cleanest existing analogue for "automated per-criterion scoring feeding a synthesised credibility score," directly transferable as a *design pattern* for the methods-appropriateness → credibility-framework pipeline, even though its domain (FAIR data) is unrelated.
- **repliCATS/IDEA** (#21) — as a design pattern for structuring the appropriateness-judgement layer (explicit criteria, independent judgement, aggregation), even though the mechanism is human panels rather than LLM.

**Ignore:**
- statcheck family (#1–6) — solve a genuinely different problem (numeric consistency of already-reported statistics), not methods/design extraction; irrelevant to HASS papers that rarely report APA-style NHST results at all.
- RobotReviewer (#9), CONSORT-NLP (#11), ARRIVE checker (#12), SciScore (#8) — all hard-coded to structured biomedical/clinical-trial reporting formats (fixed checklist items, RCT-specific vocabulary); none handle narrative or implicit methods description, and none judge appropriateness rather than presence.
- ASReview (#10), Elicit (#20) — screening/literature-synthesis tools, not methods/design extractors; useful for the user's *own* systematic-review-style corpus management, not as prior art for the extraction system itself.
- PRISMA2020 (#13) — diagram generation only, no text extraction.

## Build-vs-adopt verdict

**Build from scratch, informed by approach patterns found — this is not a solved problem for the user's target discipline or their target question.** Two independent axes of the search converge on the same conclusion:

1. **Discipline axis**: every mature or actively-maintained tool found (statcheck, RobotReviewer, CONSORT-NLP, ARRIVE checker, SciScore, rtransparent, PICO-extraction models) is built for structured biomedical/clinical-trial reporting. None handle narrative, often-implicit methods description of the kind routine in archaeological/fieldwork/HASS publications. This is an unclaimed niche, not a crowded one.
2. **Assessment axis**: every compliance/rigor tool found checks for **presence** of a reporting element against a fixed checklist (did they report blinding? randomisation? a CONSORT item?). None judge whether the *chosen* method is **appropriate** to the research question — which is the credibility-framework capability the user has already built. The closest conceptual relatives (repliCATS/IDEA, F-UJI) solve structurally similar problems (structured judgement aggregation; per-criterion automated scoring) in entirely different domains (replicability panels; FAIR data), and are worth borrowing design patterns from, not code.

Two very recent papers (Tan & D'Souza 2026, IRCDL; Simmons et al. 2025, Cochrane Evidence Synthesis and Methods) independently demonstrate that general-purpose LLM extraction still degrades sharply on exactly the tasks the user's system performs well on today (cross-reference binding, narrative/non-RCT study designs) — this is strong, citable evidence for a grant application that the user's session-per-pass, explicit+implicit, three-tier RDMAP methodology is addressing a genuinely open capability gap, not reinventing an existing wheel.

**Practical recommendation**: position the grant narrative around (a) discipline coverage — first systematic RDMAP-style extraction for HASS/fieldwork research, where reporting-guideline tooling has never been built because no CONSORT/STROBE-equivalent checklist exists for archaeology; and (b) assessment depth — appropriateness judgement, not compliance checking, as the qualitative leap beyond the SciScore/rtransparent/CONSORT-NLP family. Consider citing rtransparent, F-UJI and repliCATS as the nearest prior-art analogues by *mechanism*, and the Tan & D'Souza / Simmons et al. papers as evidence of the problem's current difficulty for LLMs generally.
