# Prior-art report: full-paper claims-evidence-methods (CEM) extraction for HASS/archaeology

## Executive summary

This is a partially-solved problem with a large, fragmented periphery and an empty centre. Explicit scientific-claim extraction and claim-verification-against-a-corpus (SciFact and its 2025-2026 successors: DeepVerify, MSVEC, SciClaimHunt, CliVER) is an active, crowded research area, but it targets a different task — checking a claim against retrieved evidence — not decomposing a single paper into its own internal claims-evidence-methods (CEM) argumentative structure. Argument mining has a mature methodological toolkit (SciArg, SciClaim, DISAPERE/AMPERE, Canary, MAMKit) but almost all of it is sentence-level, stale (2019-2022 vintage), and trained on computer science/biomedical corpora — none of it has been applied to archaeology or the humanities, arts, and social sciences (HASS). Scholarly knowledge-graph platforms (Open Research Knowledge Graph, ORKG; scite) decompose papers into structured units at scale, but ORKG's contribution templates are largely community/author-curated rather than autonomously extracted by a multi-pass pipeline, and scite classifies citation *stance* (supporting/contrasting/mentioning), not within-paper claim-evidence linkage. Systematic-review tooling with LLM components (Elicit, Consensus, RobotReviewer, laser.ai, DistillerSR, Covidence) is a mature commercial ecosystem, but it produces flat extraction tables against fixed PICO-style fields for biomedical randomised controlled trials (RCTs) — none of it models claims and evidence as a linked, weighted graph, and none is HASS-native. The single closest technical analogue found is ATR4CH/SEBI (Schimmenti, Pasqual, Vitali & van Erp, University of Bologna/KNAW Humanities Cluster, *Journal of Documentation*, 2026) — an LLM-based hypothesis-evidence knowledge-graph extraction pipeline applied to cultural-heritage authenticity debates — but it operates on short Wikipedia articles, uses only two categories (hypothesis, evidence) with no explicit/implicit split, no linkage-strength scoring, and no cross-reference graph, and it is not applied to full academic papers or archaeology specifically. Implicit-claim identification is explicitly flagged in the claim-detection literature as the harder, under-solved sub-problem relative to explicit claims, but no dedicated tool or benchmark targets implicit claims in full scientific papers. On the specific combination the project has built — full-paper, 8-pass, explicit-and-implicit claims, evidence-linkage-strength scoring, cross-reference graph, applied to HASS/archaeology — nothing found constitutes prior art; general-purpose LLM structured-extraction infrastructure (Google's LangExtract, Instructor) is the closest thing to a reusable substrate, but neither ships a CEM schema or a claims-evidence pipeline.

## Recommendations

**Use directly:**
- None of the candidates are ready to adopt wholesale for the project's core CEM extraction task. The closest generic infrastructure — `google/langextract` (row 28) and `567-labs/instructor` (row 29) — could be adopted as an underlying structured-output *substrate* if the project ever wants to move off bespoke prompt engineering onto a library with built-in source-grounding/validation, but this is an implementation-detail decision, not a "stop building, use this instead" decision.

**Adapt approach:**
- **SciClaim (row 12)** — read its 12-fold attribute/relation annotation schema (causal, comparative, predictive, statistical, proportional associations, with qualifications and subtypes) as a source of fine-grained relation-typing ideas for the CEM schema's evidence-linkage taxonomy, even though the underlying tool is stale and non-LLM.
- **ATR4CH/SEBI (row 33)** — read the five-step methodology and the SEBI ontology closely. It is the nearest published precedent for "LLM ensemble extracts a structured evidence-hypothesis knowledge graph from HASS-adjacent text," including its F1 evaluation approach and its explicit finding that evidence extraction is much more reliable (F1 0.95-0.97) than hypothesis/claim extraction (F1 0.65-0.75) — this is directly useful evidence for framing the grant's risk profile (claims are the hard part; the project's explicit vs. implicit claim split may be exactly the right response to that difficulty).
- **ARA (row 26)** and **ClaimFlow/Reading Between the Citations (rows 6-7)** — their typed-edge/typed-relation graph construction patterns (support/extend/qualify/refute; workflow dependency edges) are a useful source of relation-vocabulary ideas for the cross-reference and linkage-strength dimensions of the CEM schema, even though none targets within-paper claim-evidence structure.
- **RobotReviewer (row 19)** — its published human-vs-machine agreement studies (~72% concordance across multiple independent evaluations) are a useful external benchmark/comparator to cite when arguing the plausibility of automated structured extraction holding up against expert review — a precedent worth citing in the grant methodology section regardless of RobotReviewer's own staleness and narrow biomedical scope.

**Ignore:**
- Covidence, DistillerSR (rows 22-23) — pure workflow/screening platforms with bolt-on AI; least relevant of the systematic-review tools to a claims-evidence-graph approach.
- claimbuster-spotter, DISAPERE, mamkit, sciwing, papermage, grobid (rows 5, 14, 10, 31, 30, 32) — each solves a genuinely different problem (check-worthy political claims; peer-review discourse; multimodal debate argumentation; pre-LLM document processing; PDF structure parsing; bibliographic parsing) that is adjacent infrastructure at best, not competing prior art for CEM extraction itself.
- Reproscreener, UKHSA pipeline (rows 25, 27) — different target (code/data-availability signals; flat single-pass field extraction with a 42% full-accuracy ceiling) — worth citing as comparators, not worth adopting or adapting.

## Build-vs-adopt verdict

**Build, informed by approaches found — with two genuinely close readings required before finalising the schema.** Nothing in this search does full-paper, multi-pass, explicit-and-implicit, linkage-strength-scored, cross-referenced claims-evidence-methods decomposition, and nothing does it for HASS/archaeology at all. The project should continue building its own pipeline, but should (1) read ATR4CH/SEBI (row 33) closely as the nearest technical and disciplinary analogue — it is recent (2026), peer-reviewed, from a credible European digital-humanities group, and its F1 gap between evidence (easy) and hypothesis/claim (hard) extraction is directly relevant validation for the project's own design choices; and (2) read SciClaim's relation-attribute schema (row 12) for ideas on typing evidence-linkage strength/kind. For infrastructure, `google/langextract` or `instructor` (rows 28-29) are worth a look if the team ever wants source-grounded character-offset provenance without hand-rolling it, but adopting either is an implementation optimisation, not a strategic pivot — building the CEM schema, the 8-pass workflow, and the archaeology-specific extraction guidance remains squarely on the project. On positioning for the grant: the tooling landscape genuinely supports the novelty claim — explicit-claim verification and citation-stance classification are crowded and active; systematic-review extraction is a mature but narrowly biomedical, flat-table commercial ecosystem; and the specific combination of full-paper CEM-methods graph extraction for HASS/archaeology, with an explicit/implicit claim distinction, is unclaimed territory as of this search (July 2026).
