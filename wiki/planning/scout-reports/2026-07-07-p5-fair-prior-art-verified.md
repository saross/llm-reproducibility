# Prior-art report: FAIR assessment tooling (paper-side, LLM-judged, HASS/archaeology lane)

## Executive summary

FAIR assessment tooling is a genuinely crowded field on the *repository/metadata* side — at least 20 automated evaluators exist (a 2024 meta-review counts 20 tools and 1,180 individual metrics), all descending from Wilkinson et al.'s 2016 FAIR Metrics work via the FAIRsFAIR project's F-UJI. Every mature automated tool I found — F-UJI, FAIR-Checker, FAIR Enough, FAIRshake, FAIR EVA, FOOPS! — shares one architectural assumption: you hand it a persistent identifier or dereferenceable URL for a *single already-deposited object*, and it harvests metadata (via re3data, DataCite, SPARQL, content negotiation) and applies rule-based tests. **None of them start from a paper and follow an availability statement to a repository** — that is a genuinely open niche. Separately, a family of paper-side NLP/LLM detectors (DataSeer/PLOS OSI, oddpub, rtransparent, Barzooka, and a brand-new 2026 transportation-domain LLM pipeline) *do* start from the paper, but they detect the *presence* of a data/code availability statement and where it points — they do not then assess the FAIRness of the target object. Combining these two lineages — paper-side entry, LLM judgement, and full GO-FAIR sub-principle scoring, with data and code scored independently — appears to be unclaimed territory. The closest single precedent is "Fair-Way" (CIKM'25, November 2025), the first LLM-based FAIR assessor, but it still ingests structured repository metadata (Zenodo/Dryad/Hugging Face APIs), not paper text, and does not separate data from code. Nothing HASS-specific or archaeology-specific exists as automated tooling; the one archaeology/cultural-heritage initiative (FAIR+CARE Cultural Heritage Network) is a manual, in-development questionnaire, not an automated scorer. Two 2024–2025 meta-evaluation papers (Candela et al.; Patra et al.) are directly useful as validation-practice literature, since they critique exactly the tool category the user's lane sits in.

## Recommendations

- **Use directly**: None. No tool does what your FAIR lane does end-to-end (paper → availability statement → repository → 15 binary GO-FAIR sub-principles → independent data/code scores, LLM-judged, HASS domain).
- **Adapt approach**:
  - **SOMEF** (#11) and **codemetapy** (#9) — consider using their structured metadata extraction as a pre-processing step for the *code* FAIR pass, so the LLM is scoring against machine-extracted README/CodeMeta fields rather than re-deriving them from scratch. Reduces hallucination surface.
  - **oddpub / DataSeer / rtransparent / French BSO** (#12, #15, #13, #27) — their regex/NLP patterns for locating data-and-code-availability statements in running text are a proven, lower-cost alternative or pre-filter to a full LLM pass for the "does a statement exist and where does it point" sub-task, before your LLM does the harder FAIR-judgement work.
  - **Fair-Way** (#7) — read its divide-and-conquer prompt decomposition (chunking metadata into fine-grained per-indicator tasks) as a design pattern for your own prompt structure, even though its data source (repository API) differs from yours (paper text).
  - **howfairis's** 5-binary-check rubric (#8) — could serve as a fast, free, rule-based cross-check specifically for the *code* side (repository existence, licence, registry, citation file, checklist) to sanity-check LLM judgements on a subset of the 15 sub-principles.
- **Ignore**:
  - **FOOPS!** (#6) — scoped to ontologies/vocabularies, wrong object type entirely.
  - **Barzooka** (#14) — solves a different reproducibility problem (graph-type reporting), not availability/FAIR.
  - **openaire-cris-validator** (#16) — CRIS/OAI-PMH endpoint compliance, wrong layer of the stack.
  - **ARIADNE portal** (#21) — a discovery/catalogue front end, not an assessment tool.
  - **FAIRshake** (#4) — effectively unmaintained upstream, licence ambiguity on the maintained fork.

## Build-vs-adopt verdict

**Build** — which is what you have already done. The landscape confirms this was the right call: every mature automated FAIR evaluator assumes a repository PID as the entry point, and the only LLM-based precedent (Fair-Way, six months old at time of writing) shares that same repository-entry assumption and doesn't separate data from code. Nothing addresses archaeology or HASS specifically in an automated way — the one cultural-heritage initiative (FAIR+CARE Cultural Heritage Network) is a manual questionnaire still in development. Three concrete uses of what exists, informed by the literature found:

1. **Validation practice** — cite and apply the critique in Candela et al.
