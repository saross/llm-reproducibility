# Lit-scout report: automated extraction and assessment of research designs, methods, and protocols (RDMAP positioning)

## TL;DR

The landscape splits cleanly into six sub-conversations that barely cite each other: mature, crowded work on scientific-IE/methods extraction (ACL/EMNLP), study-design classification and risk-of-bias/reporting-checkers (JAMIA/evidence-synthesis), and statistical-integrity forensics (statcheck/GRIM) — versus a fast-growing but whole-paper-granularity LLM research-quality line (Thelwall et al.), and two near-empty cells: implicit/under-described-method inference and HASS/fieldwork method extraction. Top-3 must-reads for positioning RDMAP: Menke et al. 2025 full-text study-design classification (`10.1101/2025.04.23.25326300`), SciER methods/tasks entity-relation benchmark (`10.18653/v1/2024.emnlp-main.726`), and Thelwall 2024 "Can ChatGPT evaluate research quality?" (`10.2478/jdis-2024-0013`) — with RobotReviewer (`10.1093/jamia/ocv044`) as the key pre-LLM appropriateness precedent. Biggest gap: no existing system integrates all three tiers (design + method + protocol) in one hierarchy with appropriateness-for-the-question assessment, and implicit-method inference plus fieldwork/archaeology disciplines are essentially unoccupied — this is RDMAP's defensible novelty.

## Landscape

**What is published, and where.** The field is real and substantial but siloed. Three mature, journal-anchored conversations dominate:

1. **Scientific IE / methods extraction (C1)** lives in computational-linguistics venues (ACL Anthology: SciERC, SciER, wet-lab corpus) and increasingly MDPI/Springer. The task is well-formalised (entities + relations + coreference over methods/tasks/datasets), with benchmarks and leaderboards. This is the most directly relevant prior art for RDMAP's **methods tier**, and it is crowded.
2. **Evidence-synthesis automation (C3 + C5 + C8)** is centred on JAMIA, *Journal of Clinical Epidemiology*, *Research Synthesis Methods* (Wiley), *Systematic Reviews* (BMC), and the Marshall/Wallace/Cohen group. It covers study-design/RCT classification (design tier), risk-of-bias and reporting-guideline checking (an appropriateness precedent), and living databases (Trialstreamer). Mature, clinical-trial-centric, and highly cited.
3. **Statistical-integrity forensics (C4)** — statcheck, GRIM, ODDPub — sits in *Behavior Research Methods*, SAGE, and open-science outlets. It checks *internal consistency and transparency of reporting*, not method appropriateness.

Against these, the **LLM research-quality line (C6)** is the newest and fastest-moving: Thelwall's series (*Journal of Data and Information Science*, *Scientometrics*, Elsevier IPM), Liang et al. in NEJM AI, and — crucially for you — Brown et al. 2026 on ecological statistics in *Methods in Ecology and Evolution*. This cluster is the only one attempting **appropriateness/quality** judgement rather than presence/consistency, but almost entirely at **whole-paper granularity**.

**Journals vs preprints.** ~26 of 32 are peer-reviewed (conference or journal); ~6 are preprints/notes: four arXiv (rows 4, 6, 7, 8), one medRxiv (row 9, Menke), one bioRxiv (row 16, ODDPub). Two of those preprints likely have journal versions worth substituting (see Gaps: ODDPub → *Data Science Journal*; SciScore note → the *iScience* Rigor-and-Transparency-Index paper). The LLM-era sub-areas (C6, C7) are appearing in journals remarkably fast for how recent they are.

**Chains signal is deliberately conservative.** I ran backward chains on three productive seeds (Menke, Thelwall-2025, Cierco; SciER returned no reference data) and forward chains on four (statcheck, SciER, Thelwall-2024, wet-lab). Because chain depth was capped, most `Chains` values are 1; the **thematic clusters are the stronger structural signal** here. The statcheck forward chain in particular resolved almost entirely to generic open-science/replication commentary (LOW fit) and was not mined further.

## Thematic clusters

- **C1 SciIE — scientific IE / methods NER-RE (4 members: #1–4).** Dense, well-benchmarked, CL-venue. SciERC (#2) is the schema ancestor; SciER (#3 [sic #1]) is the current dataset/method/task benchmark. This is where to position and evaluate RDMAP's methods-tier extraction.
- **C2 ProtoX — protocol/procedure extraction (4 members: #5–8).** Coherent but **domain-locked to wet-lab and materials synthesis**; models protocols as action graphs / step sequences. Directly informs RDMAP's protocols tier, but there is no fieldwork or social-science protocol corpus here.
- **C3 DesignClf — study-design classification (5 members: #9–13).** A convergent, clinical conversation (RCT identification → multi-design/publication-type tagging). Menke 2025 (#9) is the frontier: full-text features, 61 labels, open code. This is the closest prior art to RDMAP's design tier.
- **C4 StatForensics — statistical-integrity forensics (3 members: #14–16).** High-citation landmarks that check *reporting consistency/transparency*. Relevant to the credibility framework as context, but they do not extract methods or judge appropriateness.
- **C5 RoB/Rigor — automated RoB, reporting-guideline & rigor checkers (6 members: #17–22).** The densest and most directly analogous cluster to RDMAP's appropriateness layer. RobotReviewer (#17) judges methodological quality (bias); CONSORT-NLP (#19) and AutoReporter (#20) check reporting completeness against normative checklists; SciScore (#21) detects rigor-criterion presence. All pre-LLM or non-LLM except AutoReporter.
- **C6 LLM-Quality — LLM research-quality / appropriateness (6 members: #23–28).** The convergent recent conversation and RDMAP's nearest capability precedent. Whole-paper granularity dominates; per-method appropriateness is largely absent. #28 (ecology) is the lone fieldwork-adjacent instance.
- **C7 LLM-Extract — LLM data extraction for evidence synthesis (2 members: #29–30).** LLMs extracting study characteristics (including methods) with accuracy evaluations. Adjacent to your methods tier but framed as data-extraction-for-SR, not appropriateness.
- **C8 Landscape — SR-automation reviews (2 members: #31–32).** Orientation/survey material; useful for your related-work framing and for finding further tools.

## Suggested reading (tiered)

**Tier 1 — read first (HIGH fit; specific argument each serves).**

- **Menke et al. 2025** (`10.1101/2025.04.23.25326300`) — the design-tier precedent: automated identification of study design from *full text* (not just abstracts), multi-label, with open code/data. Position RDMAP's design tier directly against it.
- **SciER, Zhang et al. 2024** (`10.18653/v1/2024.emnlp-main.726`) — the methods-tier task twin: entity+relation extraction of Datasets/Methods/Tasks from full scientific documents. Borrow/benchmark against its relation schema.
- **SciERC, Luan et al. 2018** (`10.18653/v1/d18-1360`) — the foundational scientific-IE annotation schema (entities+relations+coreference); the design precedent every later methods-IE system builds on.
- **Kulkarni et al. 2018 wet-lab corpus** (`10.18653/v1/n18-2016`) — the canonical protocols-tier corpus (steps as actions/entities/relations); the operational model for RDMAP's protocols tier.
- **RobotReviewer, Marshall et al. 2016** (`10.1093/jamia/ocv044`) — the strongest *pre-LLM* automated appropriateness/quality judgement (risk-of-bias with supporting-sentence rationale). RDMAP's appropriateness tier must be positioned against it.
- **CONSORT-NLP, Wang et al. 2020** (`10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2020.14661`) — precedent for checking explicit-method *completeness* against a normative checklist (RDMAP explicit-item coverage).
- **AutoReporter, Chen et al. 2026** (`10.1093/jamia/ocaf223`) — the most current competitor: AI automating reporting-guideline adherence assessment. Essential for a "state of the art in 2026" claim.
- **SciScore, Bandrowski et al. 2022** (`10.14293/s2199-1006.1.sor-.pppxbqn6.v1`) — presence/absence detection of rigor criteria; precedent for RDMAP explicit-method presence detection and rigor scoring. (See Gaps: cite the *iScience* RTI paper alongside/instead.)
- **Thelwall 2024 & 2025** (`10.2478/jdis-2024-0013`, `10.2478/jdis-2025-0011`) — the seminal empirical demonstration that LLMs can score research *quality*; foundational for RDMAP's appropriateness feasibility and its limitations.
- **Liang et al. 2024** (`10.1056/aioa2400196`) — the largest empirical study of LLM feedback on papers, including methodological critique, with human-overlap metrics; the benchmark for RDMAP's LLM appropriateness feedback.
