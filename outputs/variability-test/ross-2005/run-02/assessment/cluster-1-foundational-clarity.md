## Signal 1: Comprehensibility

**Score:** 78/100 (Good)

**Approach anchors applied:** abductive

### Assessment

The paper demonstrates good comprehensibility for abductive interpretive scholarship. The central explanatory claim — that the Iliad exhibits differential linguistic treatment of Akhaians (unified) versus Trojans (diverse), revealing a nascent proto-Panhellenic identity — is explicitly stated in the abstract and developed systematically through the argument. The theoretical framework draws on established scholarship regarding oral tradition preservation, Panhellenic identity formation, and the developmental timing of the Iliad's stabilisation.

The argument structure is clearly traceable: the paper establishes two foundational premises (physical things more durable in oral tradition than abstractions; linguistic arrangements reflect contemporary poet-audience worldview), then develops textual evidence from the Iliad, contrasts with Odyssey and Hymns, and infers the best explanation for the observed patterns. Key concepts like "barbarophonos," "Panhellenic," and linguistic diversity are well-grounded in classical philology conventions.

Alternative explanations are articulated — the paper explicitly addresses the possibility of strict Panhellenic/barbaros dichotomy and explains why the evidence doesn't support it, instead favouring a more nuanced "proto-Panhellenism" interpretation. Some interpretive leaps (e.g., connecting linguistic patterns to identity formation) require domain knowledge to fully evaluate, which is appropriate for disciplinary scholarship but reduces accessibility.

### Evidence

**Strengths:**
- C022: "The differential treatment of linguistic diversity reveals a nascent, non-oppositional proto-Panhellenism based on Akhaian homogeneity" — Central claim explicitly bounded with clear scope
- RD001: Two stated premises establish framework: oral tradition preservation characteristics and stabilisation timing
- C001-C002: Core dichotomy (Akhaian unity vs Trojan diversity) clearly articulated

**Weaknesses:**
- IA006: "Comparative analysis method assumed appropriate for cross-corpus interpretation" — Methodological assumptions implicit rather than defended
- Some domain-specific concepts (barbarophonos, Panhellenic) require specialist knowledge to evaluate

### Scoring Rationale

Score of 78 falls in Good band (60-79) for abductive research. Explanatory claims are stated (meets 60-79 criterion), framework is mostly clear through the two stated premises, some alternatives are mentioned and addressed, and inference logic is traceable from textual observation to proto-Panhellenism conclusion. Falls short of Excellent (80-100) because theoretical framework is implicit in disciplinary conventions rather than explicitly articulated, and not all alternative explanations are fully evaluated. Score reflects strong argument clarity with moderate framework explicitness.

## Signal 2: Transparency

**Score:** 58/100 (Moderate)

**Approach anchors applied:** abductive

### Assessment

Transparency is moderate, reflecting the genre conventions of interpretive classical philology rather than methodological weakness per se. The theoretical framework for abductive inference (interpreting linguistic patterns as evidence of identity formation) is implicit rather than explicit. The paper does not include a formal Methods section — standard for 2005 classical philology essays — and methodological choices are embedded in the argument narrative.

Evidence selection criteria are partially transparent: the paper explains why certain passages are selected (barbarophonos usage, Catalogue of Ships, divine speech patterns) but doesn't systematically justify what evidence was excluded or considered but rejected. The reasoning process is traceable but relies on shared disciplinary conventions. Source materials (Homeric texts, comparative evidence from Odyssey and Hymns) are universally accessible through standard critical editions.

The paper states two interpretive premises explicitly (RD001), which provides more framework transparency than typical for the genre. Limitations are implicit rather than stated — there's no explicit acknowledgement of what the argument cannot demonstrate or where interpretation is more speculative.

### Evidence

**Strengths:**
- RD001: "Two stated premises govern interpretation" — Framework partially explicit through stated premises
- E001-E016: Evidence sourced from canonical texts accessible through standard editions
- M001-M002: Close reading and comparative textual analysis implicit but traceable

**Weaknesses:**
- No formal methodology section (genre convention but affects transparency score)
- IM_M001: "Implicit method: textual comparison using critical apparatus conventions" — Philological conventions not documented
- No limitations section; inferential boundaries implicit
- Evidence selection criteria not systematically justified

### Scoring Rationale

Score of 58 falls in Moderate band (40-59) for abductive research. Framework is implicit but partially inferable from stated premises (meets "framework implicit or partial" criterion); alternatives are limited (some addressed, not comprehensive); evidence criteria are unclear but reasoning is partially documented through the argument structure. Falls short of Good (60-79) because framework is not fully stated, limitations are not explicitly acknowledged, and evidence selection rationale is incomplete. Score reflects adequate reasoning traceability with significant transparency gaps characteristic of pre-FAIR humanities scholarship.

## Cluster Synthesis

**Overall Foundational Clarity:** Adequate

The paper demonstrates stronger comprehensibility (78) than transparency (58), creating an asymmetric profile. The argument itself is clear and well-structured — readers can follow the inferential chain from textual evidence to proto-Panhellenic interpretation. However, the research design and methodological choices are implicit rather than documented.

This pattern is characteristic of interpretive humanities scholarship, particularly classical philology in the pre-FAIR era (2005). The paper assumes readers share disciplinary conventions regarding close reading, textual comparison, and interpretive inference. These conventions constitute a "tacit methodology" that enables comprehensibility for specialist audiences while reducing transparency for external evaluation.

### Pattern Summary

Good comprehensibility (clear argument structure, explicit claims, traceable reasoning) combined with moderate transparency (implicit methodology, disciplinary conventions assumed, limited framework documentation). This divergence reflects genre conventions rather than methodological weakness.
